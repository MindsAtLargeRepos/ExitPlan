# Chapter 3: Land Selection

## The Land Search: More Than Just Real Estate

Finding the right piece of land for your homestead isn't just about real estate—it's about finding a place where your vision can take root. This is where your dreams meet reality, and it can feel both exciting and overwhelming.

I want to be honest with you: the land search is often more emotional than people expect. You might fall in love with a property that doesn't make practical sense. You might find something that checks all the boxes but doesn't feel right. You might get discouraged when properties don't match what you imagined.

That's all normal. The key is to understand what you're really looking for—not just what you think you should want, but what actually matters to you. That's what this chapter is about: helping you discover your land preferences so you can search with clarity and confidence.

## What Are You Really Looking For?

Before we dive into checklists and criteria, let's step back and think about what you're really seeking. This isn't just about acreage or price—it's about finding a place where you can build the life you envision.

Think about your vision from Chapter 1. What kind of life are you trying to create? Is it about complete isolation and self-reliance? Or is it about living closer to nature while staying connected to community? Are you seeking a place to grow food and raise animals, or a peaceful retreat where you can live simply?

Your land needs to support that vision. A property that's perfect for someone else might be wrong for you, and that's okay. The goal is to find what's right for *you*.

### Reflection Exercise: Your Land Vision

Take a few minutes and imagine yourself on your future property. Not the specific details yet—just the feeling.

- How does it feel? Peaceful? Energizing? Secure? Free?
- What do you see when you look around? Open fields? Forest? Mountains? Rolling hills?
- What do you hear? Silence? Birds? Wind? Water?
- What's the energy like? Isolated? Connected? Wild? Tamed?

Now, think about what you need from the land to support your vision. Not what you want—what you *need*. There's a difference.

## Understanding Location: It's Personal

Location is one of the most important factors, and it's deeply personal. What works for one person might not work for you. Let's explore what matters to you.

### Climate and Growing Season

The climate you choose will affect almost everything about your homestead: what you can grow, how you'll heat and cool your home, how you'll manage water, and even your daily life.

Think about your preferences:

**Temperature:** Do you want four distinct seasons, or do you prefer milder weather? Can you handle hot summers or cold winters? What feels comfortable to you?

**Growing Season:** How long do you need your growing season to be? This depends on what you want to grow. Some people are happy with a 90-day season for basic vegetables. Others need 150+ days for a wider variety of crops.

**Precipitation:** Do you need regular rainfall, or can you work with less? Are you comfortable with drought conditions, or do you need reliable water from the sky?

**Snow:** Can you handle significant snowfall, or do you prefer minimal snow? This affects access, heating needs, and daily life in winter.

## How Much Land Do You Actually Need?

This is a question I hear a lot, and the answer is: it depends on your vision. You don't need 40 acres to homestead successfully. Some people do amazing things on 1/3 of an acre. Others need 10+ acres for their vision.

Let's think about what you actually need based on what you want to do:

**Micro-Homestead (1/3 to 1/2 acre):**
- Intensive gardening
- Small livestock (chickens, maybe a few goats)
- Small-scale food production
- Good for: People who want to maximize production on minimal land

**Small Homestead (1-3 acres):**
- Larger garden and orchard
- More livestock options (goats, sheep, maybe a pig)
- More space for systems and structures
- Good for: Most homesteading visions

**Medium Homestead (5-10 acres):**
- Extensive gardens and orchards
- Larger livestock (cattle, horses)
- More room for expansion
- Good for: People who want room to grow

**Large Homestead (20+ acres):**
- Commercial-scale production
- Extensive livestock operations
- Significant self-sufficiency
- Good for: People with ambitious production goals

## The Practical Stuff: What Really Matters

Now let's talk about the practical criteria that can make or break a property. These aren't exciting, but they're essential.

### Water: The Non-Negotiable

Water is the most important factor. Without reliable water, nothing else matters. You can't grow food, raise animals, or live comfortably without it.

**Water Sources:**
- **Well:** Reliable, but drilling can be expensive ($5,000-$15,000+)
- **Spring:** Great if you have one, but not common
- **Rainwater:** Works in areas with adequate rainfall, requires storage
- **Municipal:** Convenient, but you're dependent on the system
- **Hauling:** Possible but not sustainable long-term

**Questions to Ask:**
- Is there existing water on the property?
- If not, can you drill a well? (Check local regulations)
- What's the average well depth in the area? (Affects cost)
- Is rainwater collection legal? (It's not everywhere)
- What's the water quality like?

### Soil: The Foundation of Food Production

If you're planning to grow food, soil quality matters. Poor soil can be improved, but it takes time and money.

**Soil Considerations:**
- **Type:** Clay, sandy, loam, rocky—each has pros and cons
- **Drainage:** Poor drainage can cause problems
- **pH and nutrients:** Can be tested and amended
- **Depth:** Shallow soil limits what you can grow

**Questions to Ask:**
- What's the soil type? (Can often find in soil surveys)
- Has the soil been tested?
- Is there good soil for gardening, or will you need raised beds?
- Is the land suitable for your intended use?

### Sun Exposure: For Solar and Gardens

Sun exposure matters for two reasons: solar power and food production.

**For Solar:**
- You need good southern exposure (in the Northern Hemisphere)
- Trees or structures that block sun are a problem
- Slope direction matters

**For Gardens:**
- Most vegetables need 6-8 hours of sun daily
- Some areas can be shaded, but you need sunny spots

**Questions to Ask:**
- Is there good southern exposure for solar?
- Are there areas with full sun for gardening?
- Will trees need to be cleared?
- What's the slope and aspect?

### Access: Can You Actually Get There?

This seems obvious, but it's worth thinking about carefully.

**Access Considerations:**
- **Road type:** Paved, gravel, dirt, or no road?
- **Maintenance:** Who maintains the road? (You? County? HOA?)
- **Year-round:** Can you access it in all seasons?
- **Private road:** Are there easements or shared road agreements?

**Questions to Ask:**
- Is there legal access? (Don't assume)
- What's the road condition?
- Who maintains it?
- Is it passable in winter/rainy season?
- Are there any access restrictions?

## Zoning and Regulations: The Boring but Critical Stuff

I know—zoning and regulations aren't exciting. But they can make or break your plans, so we need to talk about them.

### Zoning: What Are You Allowed to Do?

Zoning determines what you can and can't do on your property. This varies by location, so you need to check.

**Key Questions:**
- What's the zoning classification?
- Are you allowed to have livestock? (Chickens? Goats? Pigs?)
- Can you build the type of structure you want?
- Are there minimum square footage requirements?
- What are the setback requirements?

**Red Flags:**
- Zoning that doesn't allow your intended use
- Restrictions on livestock
- Minimum square footage requirements that are too high
- HOA restrictions that limit your plans

Building codes vary widely. Some areas are very strict; others are more lenient.

**Key Questions:**
- What are the building permit requirements?
- Can you build yourself, or do you need licensed contractors?
- Are tiny homes legal?
- Are off-grid structures allowed?
- What inspections are required?

**Red Flags:**

- Requirements that don't align with your budget

### Water Rights: Know Before You Buy

Water rights are complex and vary by state. This is important if you're planning to use well water or surface water.

**Key Questions:**
- Can you drill a well? (Some areas restrict this)
- Do you have water rights? (In some states, water rights are separate from land)
- Is rainwater collection legal? (It's not everywhere)
- What are the water usage restrictions?

**Red Flags:**
- No ability to drill a well

## Property Evaluation: Putting It All Together

When you're looking at a property, you need to evaluate it systematically. But here's the thing: it's not just about checking boxes. It's about understanding how the property fits your vision.

### The Emotional vs. The Practical

You might fall in love with a property that doesn't make practical sense. Or you might find something that checks all the boxes but doesn't feel right. Both the emotional and the practical matter.

**The Emotional:** Does it feel right? Can you see yourself there? Does it inspire you?

**The Practical:** Does it meet your needs? Can you afford it? Will it work for your plans?

The best properties have both. But if you have to choose, the practical usually wins—you can't build a life on a property that doesn't work, no matter how much you love it.

## Due Diligence: Don't Skip This

Due diligence is the process of verifying everything about a property before you buy it. It's not exciting, but it's essential. Skipping due diligence is how people end up with expensive problems.

### Before Making an Offer

- Verify zoning and permitted uses
- Check property tax records
- Research water rights and access
- Check for liens or encumbrances
- Verify property boundaries
- Research local regulations

### During Escrow (If Your Offer is Accepted)

- Professional property survey
- Soil testing (if not done)
- Water testing (if well exists)
- Well flow test (if applicable)
- Perc test for septic (if needed)
- Title search
- Environmental assessment (if needed)
- Final walkthrough

## Red Flags: When to Walk Away

Some issues are fixable. Others aren't. Here are red flags that should make you think twice:

- No water access or uncertain water rights
- Zoning doesn't allow your intended use
- Extremely high property taxes
- Poor or no road access
- Flood zone or serious drainage problems
- Environmental contamination
- Unclear property boundaries
- Restrictive covenants that limit your plans
- No ability to get building permits

This doesn't mean you should walk away from every property with issues. But serious problems should give you pause, especially if they're expensive or impossible to fix.

## Your Land Search Strategy

Now that you know what you're looking for, let's talk about how to find it.

### Where to Look

- Online real estate sites (LandWatch, LandHub, etc.)
- Local real estate agents (especially those who specialize in land)
- County land sales
- Word of mouth
- Driving around target areas

### What to Do When You Find Something

1. **Research first:** Don't visit until you've done basic research
2. **Visit multiple times:** See it in different conditions (weather, time of day)
3. **Talk to neighbors:** They often know things about the property
4. **Verify everything:** Don't take anyone's word for it
5. **Take your time:** Don't rush into a decision
